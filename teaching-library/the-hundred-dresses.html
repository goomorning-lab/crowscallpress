Overview

About This Compendium

This compendium provides the book-specific analytical support for the The Hundred Dresses Lexis-level workbook. It is designed to function in conjunction with the Crow's Call Methodology and offers structured exemplar responses, clarifying notes, and interpretive guidance aligned with each section of the student text.

The CROW framework (Collect → Reframe → Oppose → Wield) establishes the instructional architecture used across levels and literary works. This document applies it directly to the novel at hand, grounding abstract instructional principles in concrete textual analysis.

Use of This CompendiumThis document is intended as a professional reference. Instructional principles define evaluative standards. Exemplar responses model clarity without limiting defensible alternatives. Teachers are encouraged to adapt discussion depth while maintaining alignment with the Crow's Call analytical framework.

Section 01

Literary Elements

Instructional Principle

Literary elements function structurally. Students must correctly identify elements, explain how they create tension, meaning, or development, and move beyond summary into structural interpretation.

Setting

Physical Location

Where the story takes place

Room 13 in a small American town school.

The single classroom environment creates a contained social system where peer hierarchy and conformity operate without interruption. The limited space intensifies the teasing because there is no alternative social environment to counterbalance it.

Chronological Time

When the story takes place

Mid-20th century (pre-digital, pre-civil rights reform era).

The historical context reflects a period when immigrant families were more visibly marginalized and social mobility was limited. The absence of modern communication heightens the permanence of social decisions, such as leaving town.

Society & Values

Cultural context

Conformity, appearance-based status, and suspicion of outsiders.

The classroom culture values popularity and material display, positioning clothing as a visible marker of belonging. Wanda's differences become socially weaponized, reinforcing the theme of prejudice.

Plot Arc

Beginning — Exposition

Wanda Petronski wears the same faded blue dress each day. She claims she owns one hundred dresses at home. Peggy leads the class in teasing her, while Maddie participates silently.

Structural Note: The "one hundred dresses" claim functions as both social trigger and narrative seed, establishing the conflict between perception and truth.

Middle — Rising Action

The teasing continues under the guise of humor. Maddie feels increasing private guilt but does not intervene. A school drawing contest for dresses and motorboats is announced. Wanda stops coming to school. The class learns that Wanda has won first place for her one hundred detailed dress designs.

Climax

Wanda's father's letter announces that the family has moved away due to the discrimination she endured.

Justification: The climax transforms teasing into irreversible consequence. The social conflict reaches its highest point when harm becomes permanent and direct reconciliation is no longer possible.

End — Resolution

The class recognizes Wanda's artistic talent only after she is gone. Peggy and Maddie attempt to find Wanda, but have no success. They decide to mail a letter. Wanda kindly writes the class and gifts her drawings to them. Maddie resolves to never stand by silently again.

Interpretive Note: The resolution restores moral balance through reflection and forgiveness, but preserves the cost of exclusion, emphasizing growth rather than reversal.

Foreshadowing

Wanda's Repeated Claim of "One Hundred Dresses"

Her calm insistence, despite ridicule, hints that her statement carries truth beyond what the class assumes.

Maddie's Private Unease

Her internal discomfort during the teasing prepares readers for her later moral reckoning.

Wanda's Increasing Absences

Early missed school days quietly signal that withdrawal — social or physical — is approaching.

Wanda's "Funny Name"

Early attention to her unfamiliar name quietly prepares readers for the later revelation that prejudice drives her family's departure.

Structural FunctionForeshadowing works by embedding outsider-status as ordinary classroom logic — through casual "othering" (name, belonging, visibility), then through absence that no one notices — so the eventual move feels like consequence rather than surprise.

Point of View

Narrative perspective

Third-person limited, primarily aligned with Maddie. The narrative filters events through Maddie's perceptions, allowing readers to experience guilt, hesitation, and moral growth internally. Wanda remains partially inaccessible, reinforcing her outsider position.

Tone

Emotional register

Quietly reflective with restrained moral gravity. Created through simple, direct prose reflecting child perspective, emotional restraint in describing bullying, and a gradual shift from humor to regret.

Section 02

Archetypes

Instructional Principle

Archetypes should not be treated as personality labels. They represent motivational drives within the Order / Freedom / Ego / Social framework.

True Shadow: Cruelty

The True Shadow in The Hundred Dresses is Cruelty — ridicule disguised as harmless play that normalizes exclusion and erodes moral responsibility. The classroom sustains this cruelty through laughter and silence, allowing prejudice to operate without immediate accountability.

Characters

Wanda Petronski
Innocent · Daydreamer
Endures ridicule without retaliation and channels humiliation into imaginative creation, allowing artistic expression to quietly overturn social judgment.
Jan Petronski
Caregiver · Parent
Acts decisively to remove his family from harm, shifting the conflict from endurance within the system to withdrawal from it.
Maddie (Madeline)
Everyman · Silent Majority
Represents passive complicity; her internal moral conflict exposes how silence sustains injustice.
Peggy
Jester · Shadow Hater
Initiates and normalizes teasing through humor, converting ridicule into a socially accepted routine.
Miss Mason
Sage · Mentor
Establishes structured fairness through the contest, unintentionally revealing the imbalance embedded in classroom culture.
The Students
Everyman · Shadow Follower
Amplify teasing through repetition and laughter, demonstrating how group behavior intensifies individual harm.

Objects / Settings

The Hundred Dresses
Magician · Visionary
Transforms humiliation into imaginative truth, revealing true value.
Boggin Heights
Outlaw · Misfit
Marks physical and social separation, reinforcing Wanda's outsider status within the town hierarchy.
Room 13
Innocent · Traditionalist
Maintains established social norms, resisting difference through routine expectations of sameness.

Section 03

Character Maps

Instructional Principle

Character analysis requires internal logic. Students must distinguish between goal and motivation, identify values and traits supported by textual behavior, and explain development using structural evidence.

Wanda Petronski

Innocent · Daydreamer Round / Dynamic

She wants to endure school without surrendering her dignity.

She longs to preserve her inner world when her outer reality is mocked and diminished.

Imagination, self-respect, quiet resilience. She values identity as something internal rather than dependent on public approval.

Positive · Creative
She channels humiliation into detailed artistic expression through her one hundred dress drawings.
Positive · Composed
She responds to teasing without retaliation, maintaining emotional control in public.
Negative · Withdrawn
She does not defend herself openly, which allows misunderstanding to persist.
Negative · Avoidant
She retreats inward rather than confronting injustice directly.
Round / Dynamic

Wanda shifts from silent endurance to quiet assertion through her artwork. Though she leaves physically, her imaginative truth overturns the class's perception, transforming how she is seen and remembered.

Jan Petronski

Caregiver · Parent Flat / Static

He wants to protect his family from discrimination and humiliation.

He refuses to allow prejudice to define his children's worth or daily safety.

Dignity, protection, family honor. He values withdrawal from injustice over participation in a hostile community.

Positive · Protective
He removes his family from the town once the teasing crosses into ethnic prejudice.
Positive · Principled
He responds through formal communication rather than retaliation.
Negative · Resolute
He makes a permanent decision without seeking reconciliation.
Negative · Unyielding
He prioritizes departure over the possibility of systemic repair.
Flat / Static

Jan remains consistent in his protective stance. His stability functions structurally as the turning point that converts classroom cruelty into irreversible consequence.

Maddie (Madeline)

Everyman · Silent Majority Round / Dynamic

She wants to belong socially without becoming the next target of ridicule.

She fears exclusion and poverty being exposed in her own life.

Acceptance, fairness, moral responsibility. She values belonging but gradually recognizes the cost of silence.

Positive · Reflective
She internally questions the teasing even while participating.
Positive · Conscientious
She resolves to change her behavior after recognizing harm.
Negative · Passive
She allows bullying to continue by remaining silent.
Negative · Fearful
She prioritizes self-protection over immediate moral action.
Round / Dynamic

Maddie undergoes internal moral growth as guilt transforms into resolve. Her shift from silent complicity to conscious commitment marks the story's restorative movement.

Section 04

Allusions & Symbolism

Instructional Principle

An allusion is an indirect reference to a historical, religious, mythological, or literary source. A symbol is a recurring object or image within the text that represents a deeper abstract idea.

In-Text Allusions

Winfield Scott

Historical / Cultural
Reference
Winfield Scott, American general and national figure.
Meaning
The casual celebration of an American military hero contrasts with the rejection of Wanda's immigrant identity. The reference exposes selective patriotism, revealing how national pride can exclude those perceived as foreign.

"Polack" Pejorative

Historical / Cultural
Reference
Anti-Polish immigrant discrimination in early 20th-century America.
Meaning
The use of the ethnic slur situates the story within a broader history of immigrant marginalization, expanding the conflict beyond childish teasing into systemic prejudice and reframing the classroom as a microcosm of national intolerance.

The Dress Drawing Contest

Artistic / Fairy-Tale
Reference
Folktale traditions of hidden identity revealed through skill or craftsmanship.
Meaning
The contest functions like a fairy-tale reversal in which underestimated virtue is revealed publicly, shifting the narrative from ridicule to recognition and intensifying the moral reversal at the heart of the story.

Sapolio (early 20th-century American advertising culture); The Gettysburg Address (foundational American ideals of equality); Tiny Tim (Victorian moral literature).

In-Text Symbolism

The Hundred Dresses → Worth
  • The hundred dresses exist only in Wanda's claim before anyone sees proof.
  • The hundred dresses appear publicly when her drawings win the contest.
  • Wanda's hundred dresses leave a lasting impact after she leaves town.
Wanda's Faded Blue Dress → Poverty
  • Wanda's faded blue dress is worn to school each day.
  • It becomes the focus of the class's teasing.
  • It contrasts with the elaborate designs she creates.
The Drawing Contest → Judgment
  • The drawing contest creates a formal standard by which student work is evaluated.
  • It places Wanda's designs before the entire class for public comparison.
  • It overturns the classroom hierarchy by naming a winner based on merit rather than popularity.

Motorboats (Escape); Wildflowers (Innocence); Broken Mirrors (Self-Perception).

Section 05

Conflict Analysis

Instructional Principle

Conflict is the structural tension between opposing forces that drives the narrative forward. Students must classify the conflict type, identify what each side wants, and explain the outcome.

Wanda vs. Discrimination

Character vs. Society
Wanda's Solution
To endure ridicule without surrendering her dignity and to assert her worth through artistic proof.
Society's Solution
To enforce conformity through teasing and to reduce difference to spectacle.
Outcome
Wanda leaves town and her talent exposes the injustice too late.

Maddie vs. Staying Silent

Character vs. Self
Maddie's Solution
To act with courage and defend what she knows is right.
Self's Solution
To remain quiet to protect her own social safety.
Outcome
Maddie resolves to reject silence and change her future behavior.

Wanda vs. Irreversible Departure

Character vs. Fate
Wanda's Solution
To maintain dignity and hope for quiet acknowledgment.
Fate's Solution
To close the opportunity for reconciliation once the family leaves.
Outcome
Recognition comes only after the loss becomes permanent and the opportunity for closure disappears.

Boggin Heights vs. The Big City

Society vs. Society
Boggin Heights
To define belonging through sameness and local hierarchy.
The Big City
To offer anonymity and the possibility of acceptance beyond reputation.
Outcome
Wanda's departure suggests that mobility becomes the only path to dignity.

Peggy vs. Empathy (Character vs. Self); The Classroom vs. Difference (Society vs. Society); Maddie vs. Irreversibility (Character vs. Fate).

Section 06

Comprehension Questions

Instructional Principle

Questions are organized into three sets reflecting the narrative's structural progression. Each answer includes a model response and a scene anchor for teacher navigation.

Section 1 — Waiting for Wanda

1

Where does Wanda sit in Room 13?

Wanda sits in the next to last seat in the last row, in the corner where the rough boys sit.

Scene: Opening description of Room 13 and Wanda's seat.

2

Why does Wanda sit in the rough corner even though she is quiet and well-behaved?

She comes from Boggins Heights and walks along country roads, so her feet are usually caked with mud when she arrives at school.

Scene: Narrator's explanation of why children from Boggins Heights sit in that corner.

3

What causes Peggy and Maddie to notice that Wanda is not at school?

They had waited for Wanda on the way to school to have fun with her, but she never came, which made them late.

Scene: Peggy and Maddie waiting on the corner and finally racing off without her.

4

When Peggy and Maddie arrive late, what is the class already doing?

The class is reciting the Gettysburg Address, and Peggy and Maddie have to slip into their seats as the last lines are being said.

Scene: The two girls entering during the formal recitation.

5

What do Peggy and Maddie notice about Wanda's desk once they are in their seats?

The desk is dusty, which tells them Wanda has not been there the day before either.

Scene: Peggy and Maddie looking across the room and observing the dusty desk.

6

How does Peggy speak to Wanda during the dresses game?

Peggy speaks in a very courteous, polite tone, as though she were talking to a teacher or the principal.

Scene: Peggy asking Wanda how many dresses she has in her closet.

7

Why is Peggy's polite tone during the dresses game misleading?

It disguises mockery as friendliness, so the teasing continues while appearing to be a normal conversation.

Scene: Peggy sweetly asking about the dresses while the other girls laugh.

8

What fear begins to grow in Maddie's mind as the dresses game continues?

She worries that if Peggy stops teasing Wanda, she might start teasing Maddie instead, because Maddie is also poor.

Scene: Maddie thinking about her own hand-me-down clothes and fearing she could be next.

Section 2 — The Game Revealed

1

What keeps drawing Maddie's attention away from her schoolwork?

She keeps looking at Wanda's empty desk, which seems to be the only thing she notices on that side of the room.

Scene: Maddie sharpening her pencil and staring toward Wanda's desk.

2

How does Maddie feel about the bright blue October day once she connects it to what happened with Wanda?

The day no longer feels bright or happy to her, even though the weather itself has not changed.

Scene: Maddie recalling that the day stopped seeming like bright blue weather after Wanda's claim began the teasing.

3

How does Wanda behave when she joins the girls admiring Cecile's dress?

She approaches very slowly and hesitantly, like a timid animal ready to run, pausing with each step before moving forward.

Scene: Wanda edging toward the group on Maple Street, step by step.

4

How does Peggy react when Wanda says she has a hundred dresses?

Peggy raises her voice and calls out to the other girls, turning Wanda's quiet comment into a public announcement that makes everyone laugh.

Scene: Peggy yelling to the crowd after Wanda speaks.

5

Why does Maddie tear up the note she starts to write to Peggy?

She imagines herself becoming the next target and pictures Peggy asking her about the old, disguised hand-me-down dress she is wearing.

Scene: Maddie picturing herself in the school yard as a new target before destroying the note.

6

How does Maddie explain Peggy's behavior to herself?

She tells herself that Peggy is the best-liked girl in the room and therefore cannot really be doing something wrong.

Scene: Maddie reasoning that Peggy could not possibly do anything that was wrong.

7

When the classroom fills with Wanda's drawings, what do Peggy and Maddie realize about her earlier claim to have a hundred dresses?

They realize the claim was true, because Wanda really did have a hundred dresses and had drawn every one of them, all different and all beautiful.

Scene: Peggy and Maddie going to the blackboard and recognizing the blue and green dresses Wanda had described.

8

After Miss Mason reads the father's letter, what do Peggy and Maddie both decide to do?

They both decide to go to Boggins Heights to find Wanda and tell her she won the contest and that her drawings are beautiful.

Scene: Peggy suggesting they go see if Wanda has left town, and Maddie realizing they had the same idea.

Section 3 — Making Amends

1

How does Maddie feel as she and Peggy walk up to Boggins Heights?

She feels unhappy and wishes everything could go back to the way it was before they had made fun of Wanda.

Scene: Maddie walking up the Heights, hoping to find Wanda.

2

What does Maddie notice about the Petronskis' house when she and Peggy arrive?

The house is small and shabby but clean, with wisps of old grass along the pathway and chicken coops at the side.

Scene: Maddie observing the little white house and comparing it to Wanda's faded but clean dress.

3

Why is it painful for Maddie when she and Peggy find the Petronskis' house empty?

She realizes the family has already gone and she may never be able to tell Wanda she is sorry.

Scene: Maddie wondering how she could bear the fact that Wanda had gone and she might never make amends.

4

That night, what does Maddie decide she will do differently in the future?

She decides she will never stand by silently again when someone is being picked on, even if it means losing Peggy's friendship.

Scene: Maddie sitting up in bed and reaching her conclusion after a long time of hard thinking.

5

Why do Peggy and Maddie write a friendly letter to Wanda instead of saying sorry directly?

They mean to apologize, but when they actually write the letter it becomes a friendly note instead, without a direct admission of fault.

Scene: The narrator noting they had meant to say they were sorry but it ended up as a friendly letter.

6

In her letter to Room 13, what does Wanda give to Peggy and Maddie?

She gives Peggy the drawing of the green dress with red trimming and Maddie the drawing of the blue dress, as Christmas gifts.

Scene: Miss Mason reading Wanda's letter to the class on the last day before the holidays.

7

What does Maddie realize when she looks closely at the face in her drawing?

She realizes that Wanda drew her, because the face in the picture has Maddie's own short blonde hair, blue eyes, and wide straight mouth.

Scene: Maddie sitting on her bed and studying the drawing until she recognizes herself.

8

Why does Maddie still feel sad at the end of the story even after receiving Wanda's letter?

She knows she will never see Wanda again and can never truly make things right between them.

Scene: Maddie saying "I hope so" sadly while Peggy feels happy and relieved.

Section 07

Theme Analysis

Instructional Principle

A topic is the subject the text explores. A theme is the author's claim about that topic, expressed as a complete, universal statement. Themes must be universal, arguable, and supported by textual evidence.

Social Class

Topic
Theme
Visible poverty can distort how worth is perceived within a community.
Evidence
Wanda's single faded blue dress becomes the basis for ridicule, reducing her identity to material lack; her one hundred dress designs later overturn that judgment, revealing value that was previously dismissed.
Contemporary
Social identity is still often inferred from visible markers such as clothing, housing, or brand ownership; the narrative invites reflection on how quickly appearance is equated with capability or character.
Similar Work
A Christmas Carol by Charles Dickens, which examines how economic status shapes perception and moral judgment.

Prejudice Against Outsiders

Topic
Theme
Communities often define belonging by excluding those who appear different.
Evidence
Wanda's Polish name and residence in Boggin Heights mark her as socially separate, and the resultant teasing escalates into ethnic targeting, prompting her departure.
Contemporary
Modern societies continue to wrestle with how language, accents, and cultural background influence acceptance or marginalization.
Similar Work
Wonder by R.J. Palacio, which explores how visible difference exposes the tension between empathy and conformity.

Forgiveness

Topic
Theme
Recognition of harm does not erase consequence, but it can reshape moral understanding.
Evidence
Maddie's guilt emerges only after Wanda leaves town, making reconciliation impossible; Wanda's kind response reframes the conflict without reversing its outcome.
Contemporary
In many contexts, apology and accountability occur after damage is done, raising questions about what restoration truly means.
Similar Work
The Little Prince by Antoine de Saint-Exupéry, which examines responsibility and the lasting impact of neglect or misunderstanding.
